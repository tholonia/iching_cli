# A Week of Open War: How the Kurdish Offensive Shattered the Iran Ceasefire Talks

**By Staff Reporter | March 12, 2026**

---

What began seven days ago as a carefully coordinated ground incursion has since unraveled into the most significant expansion of the Iran conflict since hostilities opened — pulling four governments into direct confrontation, collapsing a nascent diplomatic back-channel, and leaving the future of the region's borders an open question for the first time in a generation.

The Kurdish offensive that crossed into western Iran on March 5 did not stay limited. Within 48 hours of the initial incursion, Iranian Revolutionary Guard units had redeployed from their northern positions to contest the border corridor, abandoning pressure on Azerbaijani territory to address the new front. Kurdish commanders, reading the redeployment as confirmation that their gambit had succeeded, pushed deeper. By March 7 they held three border towns and were receiving resupply through Iraqi Kurdistan with what U.S. officials described only as "logistical facilitation."

Tehran's response, when it came, was not measured.

Iranian ballistic missile strikes hit Kurdish assembly points inside Iraqi territory on March 8, killing an estimated 34 fighters and wounding more than 90. Iraq's government, caught between its Iranian neighbor and its Kurdish north, declared a state of emergency and demanded an immediate halt to all foreign military operations on its soil — a demand that went unacknowledged in Washington, Erbil, and Tel Aviv alike.

The strikes on Iraqi territory were the breaking point for the diplomatic track. A senior European mediator confirmed Thursday that the informal ceasefire discussions that had been proceeding through Swiss intermediaries were formally suspended following the Iraqi strikes. "Both parties came to the table with maximum positions and no flexibility," the mediator said, speaking on condition of anonymity. "The Kurdish operation made any mutual de-escalation structurally impossible."

Privately, U.S. officials expressed frustration that the offensive had been timed without full interagency coordination. "This was not our calendar," one senior administration source told reporters. The White House declined to comment.

On the ground, the picture remained volatile as of Thursday evening. Kurdish forces held their positions but had not advanced further, describing their posture as "consolidating gains." Iranian forces had not attempted a direct ground counterofferation but continued missile and drone strikes against supply lines. Two Maersk-contracted cargo vessels diverted from Gulf routes reported near-miss drone incidents, and Lloyd's of London raised war-risk premiums for the entire eastern Mediterranean corridor for the second time in a week.

Analysts cautioned against reading the current stalemate as stability. "What you have now is two forces in direct contact with grievances that predate this conflict by decades," said a regional security researcher at the International Crisis Group. "The ceasefire talks didn't fail because of a miscommunication. They failed because neither side believes the other will honor any agreement reached under these conditions. That's not a negotiating problem. That's a structural one."

In Erbil, Kurdish political leaders held a press conference Thursday morning that was notable for its restraint. No territorial claims were announced. No victory was declared. The senior official present said only that the Kurdish people had "exercised a right long deferred" and that they remained "open to a political process that reflects the realities now established."

Whether those realities hold — or whether the next week brings the confrontation to a still sharper edge — remained, as of press time, unresolved.
