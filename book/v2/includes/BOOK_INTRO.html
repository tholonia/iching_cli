The Tholonic Model and the I Ching

The I Ching, like the Tholonic Model, is based on archetypal structures that describe transformation and interaction. Each hexagram represents a dynamic process, unfolding through six stages that mirror the Tholonic cycle of emergence, growth, and completion. The yin-yang duality of the I Ching aligns with the Tholonic Model's negotiation, limitation, and contribution, revealing a shared understanding of change as a structured yet fluid process.

By interpreting the I Ching through the Tholonic perspective, we uncover a profound self-organizing intelligence within its structure. Each hexagram is not merely a divinatory tool but a map of awareness shaping reality—a guide to understanding how intention, form, and balance operate at every level of existence.

This book presents a reimagined I Ching, applying the Tholonic Model to deepen our engagement with its wisdom. Through this lens, we see that every question, challenge, and transformation is part of a larger, interconnected pattern—one that we, through awareness and intention, actively shape.

Both the I Ching and the Tholonic model propose that there are archetypes of form and interaction that manifest differently depending on the context and scope. These archetypes lead to an almost infinite number of situations, but the underlying pattern remains the same.

In the I Ching, these archetypes can be understood using the concept of two groups of three yin and yang (trigrams), applied to six fundamental phases representing the general life cycle of creation, growth, and completion both 'internally' and 'externally', however these concepts are defined given the context. These phases are:

  1. Conception/Initiation (Hidden Potential): The phase where potential exists but is not yet manifested. It involves preparation, gathering energy, and setting intentions for creation. In the I Ching, this corresponds to the Hidden Dragon, representing latent power and readiness.

  2. Emergence (Early Development): The initial manifestation where something takes form, moving from potential into reality. This phase involves foundational work, like the Dragon in Fields stage in the I Ching.

  3. Expansion/Growth (Building Power): Growth accelerates, and creative energy is fully engaged. Progress is visible as activity increases. This is represented by the Active Dragon phase in the I Ching, symbolizing the active pursuit of creation.

  4. Peak/Flourishing (Maturity and Mastery): The creation reaches its full potential, with energy and activity at their peak. In the I Ching, this corresponds to the Flying Dragon, representing mastery and accomplishment.

  5. Consolidation (Completion): Growth stabilizes, and the focus shifts to maintaining or harvesting the creation. Refinement and careful resource use ensure lasting impact. This phase is represented by the Leaping Dragon, preparing for the next cycle.

  6. Transition/Transformation (Transcendence and Renewal): The final phase, where creation either ends or transforms. It signifies the end of a cycle and often brings a new beginning. This is represented by the Transcendent Dragon, suggesting renewal or a new cycle.

These phases illustrate the interconnected and cyclical nature of all things, as reflected in the I Ching.

At its core, this pattern embodies the fundamental duality of nothingness and something—or 0 and 1—structured through the principle of 2 × 3, a recurring archetype in natural processes. This concept aligns closely with the Tholonic Model, which asserts that all creation arises from duality and that 2 × 3 is a foundational structure of interaction.

The Tholonic Model further demonstrates that this archetypal pattern is embedded in physical and metaphysical principles, forming the basis of Newton’s Second Law of Motion, Ohm’s Law of Electricity, and the fundamental behaviors of energy, light, and gravity. Beyond the physical realm, it extends to awareness and intention, which the Tholonic Model identifies as the origin of all existence and creation.

Six Fundamental Phases: The I Ching outlines six phases of creation, growth, and completion, which aligns with the Tholonic model's stages of development, reflecting a similar understanding of the life cycle of any phenomenon.

Interconnectedness and Cyclicality: Both systems emphasize the interconnected and cyclical nature of all things, portraying a continuous flow of creation, transformation, and renewal.

Self-Similarity: The Tholonic model's concept of self-similarity is mirrored in the I Ching's archetypal stages, where each phase embodies a similar pattern of development and transformation.

Application to Natural Processes: Both models apply their principles to natural processes, illustrating how fundamental archetypes govern the dynamics of nature, from physical laws to energy, light, and even awareness.

Universal Principles: Both the I Ching and the Tholonic model aim to describe universal principles that underlie existence, focusing on how creation emerges, evolves, and returns to its source.

When seen as a contributing element to larger system, it is called a parton.. This idea emphasizes the interconnectedness of all levels of existence, showing that every element is both influenced by and contributes to the greater structure it belongs to.

In the Thologram, each holon takes the form of a tetrahedron, embodying a self-similar and recursive structure that mirrors the organization of reality. These fundamental units, called tholons, integrate the stability of a tetrahedron with the adaptive properties of a holon, allowing them to function as both independent entities and interconnected components of a greater system.

The Thologram serves as the structural representation of the Tholonic Model, forming a recursive matrix composed of interlinked tetrahedrons. Each tholon is a distinct whole while simultaneously being part of a larger framework, creating a holarchical structure where individual units contribute to the integrity of the entire system without losing their unique characteristics. Through this framework, the Thologram illustrates how awareness and intention give rise to increasing complexity, shaping reality through an interconnected web of relationships.

Explanation of Labels and Terms Used in Each Hexagram

King Wen Sequence and King Wen Title

The King Wen Sequence refers to the traditional arrangement of the 64 hexagrams of the I Ching, along with their corresponding King Wen Titles—the names by which they are commonly known today. This sequence was developed by King Wen of the Zhou Dynasty, who is believed to have lived from 1152 BCE to 1056 BCE. The Zhou Dynasty, which followed the Shang Dynasty (c. 1600 BCE – c. 1046 BCE), was established around 1046 BCE and lasted until 256 BCE.

King Wen is credited with reorganizing the hexagrams into a structured poetic and symbolic order, which offers profound insights into the dynamic interactions of yin and yang forces. Unlike the later binary order, which arranges the hexagrams based on mathematical logic, the King Wen Sequence is deeply rooted in cultural and philosophical symbolism. It reflects the cosmological and ethical worldview of the Zhou Dynasty, emphasizing balance, transformation, and the natural cycles of existence.

Above and Below in the I Ching

The terms Above and Below in the I Ching refer to the two trigrams that form a hexagram. Each hexagram consists of two stacked trigrams, each made up of three lines that can be either yin or yang.

  • Below (Lower Trigram): This represents the foundation of the hexagram and its internal aspect. It symbolizes the root cause, underlying energy, or inner condition of a situation. The lower trigram often relates to the personal, hidden, or preparatory phase of development.

  • Above (Upper Trigram): This represents the external manifestation of the hexagram and its overarching influence. It symbolizes the visible outcome, external forces, or overarching state of a situation. The upper trigram often shows how internal conditions interact with the outer world, shaping external events or larger-scale dynamics.

Together, the upper and lower trigrams form a dynamic interaction between internal and external forces, shaping the meaning and energy of the hexagram. This interplay embodies the core principle of yin and yang, reflecting the constant balance between inner potential and outward expression in all aspects of existence.

The Trigrams and Their Origins

The structure and meaning of the upper and lower trigrams in the I Ching are deeply rooted in ancient Chinese philosophy, particularly Taoism and early Chinese cosmology. The eight trigrams, known as the Bagua (八卦), represent fundamental principles of nature, consciousness, and transformation.

Each trigram is composed of three lines of either yin (⚋) or yang (⚊), and symbolizes a distinct natural force, human quality, or cosmic principle. These trigrams interact within a hexagram to reveal deeper patterns of change, adaptation, and balance in both personal and universal contexts.

By understanding the placement and interaction of these trigrams within a hexagram, one can gain greater insight into the cycles of transformation, providing a structured way to interpret the I Ching’s wisdom on change, decision-making, and harmony.

  1. ☰ Qian (Heaven): Creativity, strength, leadership.

  2. ☷ Kun (Earth): Receptivity, nurturing, adaptability.

  3. ☲ Li (Fire): Illumination, clarity, transformation.

  4. ☵ Kan (Water): Depth, mystery, fluidity.

  5. ☶ Gen (Mountain): Stillness, meditation, grounding.

  6. ☴ Xun (Wind/Wood): Penetration, gentleness, flexibility.

  7. ☳ Zhen (Thunder): Initiative, action, arousal.

  8. ☱ Dui (Lake): Joy, openness, communication.

Philosophy/Religion Associated with the Bagua:

The Bagua serves as the foundation of Taoist philosophy and Chinese metaphysics, influencing practices such as Feng Shui, Tai Chi, and Qi Gong. In Confucian thought, the trigrams are also regarded as symbols of moral and social order, reflecting principles of harmony, duty, and balance in human life.

As a core component of the I Ching (Book of Changes)—one of the oldest texts in Chinese tradition—the Bagua is used both for divination and as a philosophical system. Its structure embodies the interplay of yin and yang, illustrating how natural forces continuously transform through cycles of balance and change. In Taoism, the Bagua is a tool for understanding the flow of energy in the universe, emphasizing the constant interaction of opposites as a driving force behind all existence.

The concept of upper and lower trigrams in the I Ching originates from this ancient philosophical system, deeply rooted in the study of cosmic balance, natural forces, and human interaction with the universe.

The Bagua, or eight trigrams, predates King Wen of the Zhou Dynasty and was already an established concebtcpt in early Chinese cosmology and philosophy. Traditionally, its origins are attributed to Fuxi (伏羲), a mythical sage-emperor believed to have lived around 2800 BCE. According to legend, Fuxi observed patterns in nature—the sky, the earth, animal movements, and environmental cycles—and codified these insights into the eight trigrams, creating a symbolic system to represent the fundamental forces of the universe.

Development and King Wen's Contribution:

Fuxi's Early Bagua Fuxi is credited with creating the "Primordial Bagua" (Xiantian Bagua, 先天八卦), a symmetrical arrangement of the trigrams that represents the natural order and balance of the cosmos. This arrangement reflects the fundamental harmony of opposites and the way forces interact within the universe.

King Wen's Role By the time of King Wen, the Bagua had already been an established system for over a millennium. King Wen expanded its application by combining the trigrams into 64 hexagrams, allowing for more complex interpretations of change and transformation. He also reorganized these hexagrams into what is now known as the King Wen Sequence, a structured arrangement that illustrates the dynamic interplay of yin and yang over time.

Thus, while the Bagua predates King Wen, his work was instrumental in developing the I Ching into a comprehensive philosophical, divinatory, and cosmological system. His arrangement provided deeper insights into patterns of existence, cycles of change, and principles of transformation, ensuring the I Ching's continued influence across cultures and eras.

Binary Sequence: The binary sequence of the I Ching hexagrams assigns a numerical value to each hexagram based on the arrangement of its lines, treating yang lines as 1 and yin lines as 0. The lines are read from the bottom to the top of the hexagram, with the bottom line representing the least significant bit and the top line representing the most significant bit.

For example ䷁ would correspond to the binary number 000000, or 0, ䷊ would be 000111, or 7 in decimal form, and ䷀ would be 111111, or 63 in decimal form.

This binary sequence provides a systematic way to number the 64 hexagrams from 0 to 63, offering a precise and logical framework for organizing the hexagrams according to their energetic composition. This sequence, often referred to as the binary order, is distinct from the King Wen sequence, as it reflects a purely numerical and structural approach rather than a symbolic or philosophical one.

A fascinating historical footnote is that the binary sequence of the I Ching hexagrams captured the interest of Gottfried Wilhelm Leibniz (1646–1716), a German philosopher, mathematician, and co-inventor of calculus. When Jesuit missionaries brought the I Ching to Europe, Leibniz encountered its hexagrams and immediately recognized their binary mathematical structure.

Leibniz observed that the I Ching's representation of yin as 0 and yang as 1 mirrored the binary system he had been developing. He was struck by its mathematical elegance, noting how this binary structure aligned with his concept of binary arithmetic. Though he admired its mathematical properties, Leibniz believed binary mathematics had little practical use in his time. Instead, he saw it as the "mathematics of God," reflecting a universal order through its simplicity and its ability to represent creation as emerging from duality—something (yang) and nothing (yin).

Ironically, while exploring binary mathematics, Leibniz was also working on one of the earliest mechanical computers. In the 1670s, he designed and built the Stepped Reckoner, a device capable of performing addition, subtraction, multiplication, and division. Based on a system of gears, this invention laid the foundation for later developments in mechanical and digital computing.

Two hundred seventy years later, the two ideas Leibniz had been developing—the "binary mathematics of God" and the foundations of computation—merged to give rise to the digital age. The binary system, embedded in the structure of the I Ching and in Leibniz's mathematical insights, now underpins all digital technology. Computers, algorithms, and digital communication systems rely on binary logic, encoding and processing information using sequences of 0s and 1s. This unintended yet profound legacy of Leibniz's work illustrates how an abstract philosophical concept evolved to shape the technological foundations of the modern world, forming a bridge between ancient Eastern wisdom, Enlightenment-era thought, and contemporary innovation.

Pairs of Archetypes:

Because each hexagram has its exact negative form, such as ䷁ and ䷀ or ䷊ and ䷋, we can view the 64 hexagram archetypes as actually representing 32 archetypes in two different contexts. The key difference between these two contexts is the state of the sixth (top) line. In one set, the sixth line is always yin, while in the other, it is always yang.

Since the sixth line represents transition and transformation, often associated with transcendence and renewal, the two contexts of hexagram pairs indicate whether the final transformation is yin or yang. This distinction provides an additional layer of meaning, framing each pair as either a yielding, receptive transformation (yin) or an assertive, active transformation (yang).

A more intuitive way to understand these two contexts is in terms of ascending and descending cycles.

The Two Contexts:

  • The Ascending Cycle: All hexagrams with binary values from 0 to 31 (where the sixth line is yin) belong to the ascending phase. These hexagrams move from a pure yin state toward a pure yang state, reflecting an increase in expansive energy, creativity, and outward expression. This phase represents growth, emergence, and creation, symbolizing the rising force of energy as it moves toward its peak.

  • The Descending Cycle: All hexagrams with binary values from 63 to 32 belong to the descending phase, moving from a pure yang state toward a pure yin state. These hexagrams indicate a decrease in expansive energy, representing contraction, dissolution, and a return to stillness or nothingness. This phase mirrors the natural decline of energy, symbolizing completion, rest, and renewal.

The terms ascending and descending reflect the natural movement of energy. Ascending from yin to yang corresponds to creation, expansion, and increasing dynamism. Descending from yang to yin corresponds to completion, return, and the transition to stillness.

The Structure of the Cycles:

Hexagrams are counted from 0 to 31 for the ascending cycle and 63 to 32 for the descending cycle because each hexagram is naturally paired with its complementary counterpart. For example, the hexagram with a binary value of 0 (pure yin) is paired with the hexagram with a binary value of 63 (pure yang). This first pair represents the fundamental duality of nothingness (yin) and something (yang), the foundation of creation. Since this pair defines the starting point, the ascending cycle progresses from 0 to 31, while the descending cycle progresses from 63 to 32, reflecting the complete transition from pure yin to pure yang and back again.

Looking at the hexagrams from this binary perspective we can see a direction of a hexagram—whether it is ascending or descending—refers to the direction of the transformation occurring within the hexagram as it transitions through states of yin (receptive, passive) and yang (active, expansive) energy.

A Universal Framework of Energy Flow:

This cyclical pattern mirrors fundamental cosmological principles found across many metaphysical traditions, such as the waxing and waning of the moon, the rise and fall of seasonal energy, and the expansion and contraction cycles seen in nature and physics.

By organizing the I Ching hexagrams according to this ascending and descending structure, we reveal a universal framework for understanding energy flow, highlighting the rhythms of emergence, growth, completion, and renewal in all aspects of existence.

The I Ching and the Kabbalah:

One of the most fascinating and insightful correlations we find in the I Ching is how itr so perfectly describe the Kabbalah, or visa versa, depending on your perspective.

Within these 32 archetypes, 10 exhibit perfect internal equilibrium, where the number of yin and yang lines is equal (three yin, three yang). These balanced hexagrams are called stable pairs because they reflect an inherent resistance to change. Their equilibrium represents foundational stability and harmony, making them symbolic of states that persist rather than transform.

The remaining 22 pairs are imbalanced, containing either more yin or more yang. These are known as dynamic pairs, representing movement, transformation, and the interplay of complementary forces. Unlike stable pairs, dynamic pairs emphasize change, evolution, and the natural cycles of growth and transition within the system.

This division of 10 stable pairs and 22 dynamic pairs mirrors the structural pattern of the Kabbalistic Tree of Life, where 10 stable sephiroth represent foundational, balanced states, and 22 dynamic paths symbolize the active, connecting forces between them. In both systems, the interplay of stability and movement forms a cosmological framework for understanding balance, transformation, and interconnection.

The correlation between the I Ching and Kabbalah is so strong that the two systems can be mapped onto one another with striking precision. The stable hexagram pairs align with the 10 sephiroth, representing foundational archetypes of existence, while the dynamic hexagram pairs correspond to the 22 paths, embodying the flow of energy, connection, and change.

This mapping deepens our understanding of both systems, revealing shared principles that transcend their cultural origins. By integrating these frameworks, we uncover universal patterns of stability and transformation, offering a broader and more interconnected perspective on balance, dynamism, and the nature of existence.

 

About the Images:

The selection of images was purely subjective. While the images were based on the content and concepts of the hexagram, there are countless ways to artistically interpret and present that content. To add coherence, the AI selected a known art style and artist whose work aligned with the content. Within these parameters, it created images influenced by these styles and artists.

This approach led to intriguing results, such as wood carvings "by" Georgia O'Keeffe, post-modernist art "by" Leonardo da Vinci, and even paintings inspired by the "style" of Jack London. While some images resemble works that could have been created by the attributed artists, none of them ever existed in reality.

The AI was given significant flexibility in how to create these images and was instructed to prioritize symbolic representation. As a result, some images, like those labeled as "turtles" or "lightning," may not visually resemble turtles or lightning. This is intentional—while the descriptions may seem confusing if you expect literal depictions, the images contain symbolic representations of these concepts, at least as interpreted by the AI's "imagination."

 

The Project

The technology and effort, behind this project was extensive. First, we contextually trained an AI model with Tholonic and I Ching concepts and research that had been collected and generated going back decades, ensuring it had a deep understanding of the framework. Next, we leveraged two advanced AI services, OpenAI and Anthropic, to develop conversational agents that could interact, analyze, and exchange ideas dynamically.

For image generation, we utilized Meta’s open-source AI model, LLaMA, running on a high-performance local machine. This model worked alongside various imaging large language models (LLMs) to create visual representations of concepts derived from the analysis.

In addition, we developed a suite of custom Python programs to sort, edit, filter, translate, process, and infer meaning from the raw data. This data was stored in a structured database, allowing for efficient retrieval and refinement. Every piece of generated content was then manually reviewed and meticulously refined to ensure accuracy and alignment with the project's overarching vision.

Inspiration credit goes to the late Ralph Gun Hoy Siu's remarkable book, Portable Dragon: The Western Man's Guide to the I Ching (originally published as The Man of Many Qualities: A Legacy of the I Ching). This extraordinary work enriches the traditional I Ching by weaving in approximately 700 excerpts from 650 writers, spanning 6,000 years and representing 60 countries. Siu's anthology draws insights from an incredible range of voices—poets, scientists, tyrants, saints, hermits, adventurers, storytellers, and literary figures—offering a universal perspective on the timeless themes of the I Ching.
